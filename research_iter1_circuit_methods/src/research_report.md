## Research Findings

Four key circuit analysis papers were surveyed in detail, extracting exact methods, metrics, and quantitative results relevant to our proposed motif spectroscopy approach.

Sun 2025 (ACL) introduces circuit stability using Spearman's rank correlation K between edge importance rankings of soft circuits [1]. The alpha-equivalence threshold (alpha=0.6 for arithmetic) determines when two circuits are structurally similar, and epsilon-circuit stability measures whether a model uses consistent circuits across task instances [1]. Tested on Gemma-2-2b (~79K edges), Phi-1.5 (~128K edges), Llama-3.1-8b (~1.5M edges), and Gemma-2-9b (~720K edges) across arithmetic, boolean evaluation, and sports understanding tasks [1]. Key quantitative results include: >20% performance gaps between commutative arithmetic pairs, 5 distinct circuit families emerging at alpha=0.6, a 40% performance drop from parentheses in boolean tasks (Phi-1.5), and chain-of-thought increasing stability with p<0.05 [1]. Implementation code with EAP-IG modifications for Group-Query Attention is available open-source [14]. Crucially, this framework compares NO competing baselines and the Spearman metric is identity-dependent -- it compares which specific edges appear, not abstract structural patterns [1].

Tigges et al. 2024 (NeurIPS) tracks circuit consistency across the Pythia model family (70M to 2.8B parameters, with supplementary 6.9B and 12B) using EAP-IG with an 80% faithfulness threshold and binary search over circuit sizes from 1 edge to 5% of model edges [2]. The primary consistency metric is EWMA-Jaccard similarity with alpha=0.5 decay [2]. Pearson correlations for circuit size vs model size are: r=0.72 for IOI and SVA, r=0.9 for Greater-Than, and r=0.6 for Gendered-Pronoun [2]. Using 154 checkpoints per model across 300B training tokens, they find induction heads emerge at 2-8x10^9 tokens, name-mover head importance exceeds 70% for IOI circuits, and larger models show higher EWMA-Jaccard scores (more stable circuits) [2]. The analysis is limited to a single model family (Pythia) and only 4 simple tasks [2]. The gap for our work: this is component-level analysis (which heads appear), not subgraph topology analysis (how they connect).

Birardi 2025 develops probe prompting for automated circuit interpretation on Gemma-2-2B-it with cross-layer transcoders containing 2.5M features [3]. The pipeline uses Claude-3.5-Sonnet (temperature 0.7) for concept generation, synthesizes context-varying probes, measures activations via the Neuronpedia API, and aggregates Cross-Prompt Activation Signatures (CPAS) [3]. Features are classified into supernode categories: Semantic (peak_consistency >= 0.80), Relationship (median_sparsity < 0.45), and Say-X output-promoting (func_vs_sem >= 50%, layer >= 7) [3]. Across 5 factual recall circuits (Dallas-Austin, Oakland-Sacramento, Michael Jordan-Basketball, Small-opposite, Muscle-diaphragm), mean completeness is 0.83 and mean replacement is 0.54 [3]. Peak-token consistency achieves 2.3x improvement over cosine baselines (0.425 vs 0.183), and 64% of features (25/39) transfer across entity swaps [3]. Full pipeline code is available open-source [15]. The gap: clusters features into supernodes by behavioral similarity but does NOT analyze the topology of connections between supernodes.

Marks et al. 2024 (ICLR 2025) discovers sparse feature circuits in Pythia-70M using SAEs (d_SAE = 64 * d_model) and integrated gradients with N=10 alpha steps [4]. Their SHIFT method for debiasing Bias-in-Bios improves worst-group accuracy from 24.4% to 89.0% (vs oracle 91.9%), profession accuracy from 61.9% to 93.1%, while reducing gender-based classification from 87.4% to 52.0% [4]. SVA circuits contain 69 features at threshold 0.1 with faithfulness of 0.19, and BiB circuits contain 67 features (55 task-irrelevant) [4]. SAE-based circuit discovery code is available open-source [16]. Critically, they anecdotally identify two parallel pathways in SVA: (1) subject number detection to verb form promotion, and (2) subject number to boundary detection to verb form [4]. These parallel pathways are exactly the type of feed-forward-loop-like motif that our census would quantify systematically rather than identify manually.

Novelty Gap Confirmation: After executing 9+ targeted search queries across multiple phrasings and thoroughly examining 30+ candidate papers, NO prior work applies Alon-style network motif analysis (subgraph census, Z-scores against random baselines, motif significance profiles, or superfamily classification) to LLM attribution or circuit graphs [5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13]. The closest prior work is Zahn et al. 2024 [5], which applies motif Z-score analysis (1000 random rewirings across 350 networks) to weight-topology graphs of pruned 4-layer MLPs (400-node hidden layers) trained on insect flight control. Zahn's work differs fundamentally in five ways: (a) weight-topology not attribution/causal graphs, (b) vanilla MLPs not transformers/LLMs, (c) no motif spectrum clustering or superfamily classification, (d) no causal validation (explicitly states 'relating connectivity patterns to function is beyond scope'), and (e) no cross-task comparison [5]. Zambra et al. 2020 similarly studies weight-topology motif emergence in MLPs under different initialization schemes (Normal, Orthogonal, Xavier), finding motifs consistent with biological transduction networks -- but again only weight-level topology, not attribution graphs [6]. Anthropic's Circuit Tracing (March 2025) constructs attribution graphs for Claude 3.5 Haiku with CLT features and identifies qualitative patterns (parallel pathways, amplification chains), but performs no systematic motif analysis, no Z-score computation, and no significance profiling [7]. Their companion biology paper similarly describes circuit patterns qualitatively without any graph-theoretic motif framework [8]. The NeurIPS 2024 deception paper uses 'motif' colloquially to mean a recurring behavioral pattern (three iterative refinement stages) across 20 LLMs, with no subgraph census or graph-theoretic analysis whatsoever [9]. Circuit Compositions (ACL 2025) explores modular structures in transformer circuits via node overlap (IoU/IoM) and cross-task faithfulness (JSD), but performs no subgraph motif census or Z-score analysis [10]. Nature Communications 2025 analyzes 882,000 three-node motifs for ANN architecture design (finding incoherent loops superior to coherent), but this is for network engineering, not LLM interpretability [11]. The mechanistic interpretability glossary defines 'motif' as a 'fuzzy notion of some abstract notion that recurs' -- confirming only colloquial usage exists in the MI field, not Alon-style graph-theoretic analysis [12]. GNN motif estimation work proposes using graph neural networks to estimate motif significance profiles via multi-target regression, but has not been applied to LLM circuits [13].

Five replicable baselines were identified for our experiments: (1) Edge-overlap similarity via Spearman rho between edge importance rankings from Sun 2025, where alpha=0.6 separates arithmetic subtask clusters [1]; (2) graph-size features (nodes, edges, density) as trivial classifiers; (3) mean attribution strength (average absolute edge weight); (4) node-count Pearson correlation from Tigges 2024, with reference values r=0.72-0.9 within-task [2]; and (5) node-set Jaccard similarity from Tigges 2024, using EWMA-Jaccard with alpha=0.5 [2]. Our motif spectrum cosine similarity should outperform all five baselines because it captures abstract topological structure independent of feature identity -- two circuits implementing the same algorithm via entirely different features would score low on all five identity-dependent baselines but high on motif similarity.
